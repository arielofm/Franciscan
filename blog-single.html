Homo Faber et Oeconomicus vs. Homo Culturalis et Religiosus

  • Home
  • /
  • Article details
Creativity 5 Comments 22nd December

A dominant image of human in our days is the vision of the "Homo Faber", the Maker, connected with the comportment of the "Homo Oeconomicus", the Economic Man.

In a general sense "Homo Faber" is the concept of human beings able to control their fate and their environment through tools. This image is now also known by the novel with the same name and written by Max Frisch, a famous Swiss novelist. But the original term is coming from the Latin literature. Appius Claudius Caecus, an ancient Roman politician, referred to the ability of man to control his destiny and what surrounds him: Homo faber suae quisque fortunae (Every man is the artifice of his destiny). In older anthropological theories, the “Homo Faber” is the worker.

In modern times, sometimes the term is also used in opposition or juxtaposition to “Deus faber” ("God the Creator"). In this case, humans see themselves as the creators of their world without the need of any God. Umberto Eco, a famous Italian philosopher and novelist, argues that the idea of the “Homo Faber” represents the alienation from and objectification of nature.

This vision of the “Homo Faber” is strongly connected to the vision of the “Homo Oeconomicus”. The vision of the “Homo Oeconomicus” is a concept in many economic theories portraying humans as systematically rational and broadly selfinterested and selfish agents who usually pursue their subjectively-defined ends optimally. Mostly the “Homo Oeconomicus” attempts to maximize utility as a consumer and profit as a producer. This mentality has the goal to achieve the maximum of profit as quickly as possible. The only aim is to make short time profit possible.

The combination between the concept of the “Homo Faber” and the “Homo Oeconomicus” are forming worldwide the framework for the Economy, the programs of work and the lifestyle.

Consciously ore unconsciously most of us are taken over by the characteristics of this framework. Many people may also feel to be captive of this framework, because this framework is creating more and more victims in our world. To mention only some of the consequences: the more and more short time repetition of economic crises with worldwide effects, the increasing gap between a small group of enriched people and countries and more and more people and countries living on the edge of the minimum of the essential for life; on the individual level stress on the level of illness, loss of orientation and sense of life, ruins of existence, relationships and families. This and many other consequences of this dominant framework show that our world needs some other anthropological vision.

Today we must diagnose that in the long run the dominant anthropological visions of the “Homo Faber” and the “Homo Economicus” are hostile to the human life, hostile to the environment and hostile to the international economy itself. Based on the theological and spiritual roots the Franciscan Anthropology offers the alternative vision of the “Homo conducibilis”, the human of common good (conducibilise). The common good which creates a positive human culture.
